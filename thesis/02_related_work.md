# Chapter 3: Related Work

The source detection problem has attracted substantial attention across several research communities over the past fifteen years. Work in this area can be grouped into three broad traditions: classical analytical and simulation-based methods that derive estimators from probabilistic first principles; supervised machine learning methods — primarily GNN-based — that operate on static projections of the contact network; and a nascent body of work that attempts to exploit temporal structure more directly. We survey each tradition in turn and conclude by identifying the research gap that this thesis addresses.

## 3.1 Classical Source Detection Methods

The foundational contribution to algorithmic source detection is the work of Shah and Zaman \cite{Shah2011}, who observed that the source of a spreading rumour on a tree-structured network can be identified as a maximum likelihood estimator under the SI model. They defined rumour centrality as the number of ways the observed infection pattern can be explained as a spreading process rooted at a given node, and showed that the node maximising this quantity — the rumour centre — is a strongly consistent estimator of the true source as network size grows. An extension to directed and more general network topologies, the Jordan centre, selects the node that minimises the maximum distance to any infected node, approximating the centroid of the infection subgraph. Both estimators are computationally efficient and do not require knowledge of the spreading parameters, but they inherit a fundamental limitation: the tree-structure assumption fails on networks with cycles, and neither method accounts for the temporal ordering of contacts.

Lokhov et al. proposed dynamic message-passing (DMP) as a principled approach to approximate the marginal probabilities of infection under the SIR model on arbitrary graphs \cite{Lokhov2014}. DMP iterates a system of equations that propagates probability messages along edges, and the inferred source is the node whose initialisation as infected maximises the likelihood of the observed snapshot. The method is fast and scales to moderate network sizes, but it requires precise knowledge of the transmission probability $\beta$ and recovery probability $\mu$, which are rarely known in practice, and it becomes inaccurate on networks with many short cycles due to the Bethe approximation underlying the message-passing equations.

The Soft Margin Estimator (SME) of Antulov-Fantulin et al. \cite{AntulovFantulin2015} takes a different approach: for each candidate source, a large ensemble of Monte Carlo SIR simulations is run, and the source score is defined as the proportion of simulations whose final snapshot is compatible with the observed one under a soft-margin criterion. The method is model-agnostic in the sense that any stochastic spreading model can be plugged in, and it is the only classical method that naturally handles partial observations. Its principal drawback is computational cost: a reliable score requires thousands of simulations per candidate node, yielding quadratic scaling in the number of candidate sources. Braunstein and Ingrosso \cite{Braunstein2016} proposed a loopy belief propagation variant that is better calibrated on networks with cycles, but at comparable or higher computational expense.

A structurally different approach is the Steiner tree method of Rozenshtein et al. \cite{Rozenshtein2016}, which reconstructs the most parsimonious propagation subgraph connecting the observed infected nodes. This model-agnostic topological approach does not require parameter knowledge but does not produce a probabilistic ranking of source candidates. Taken together, these classical methods share a common limitation for the purposes of this thesis: they were designed for static or single-snapshot contact networks and either discard or only partially exploit the temporal ordering of contacts.

## 3.2 GNN-Based Methods on Static Networks

The application of graph neural networks to source detection was initiated by Dong et al. \cite{Dong2019}, who introduced GCNSI (Graph Convolutional Network for Source Identification), framing the problem as multi-source detection on a single static network. Their key methodological contribution was the use of Label Propagation Algorithm (LPA) outputs as augmented node features, providing the GCN with a soft prior over likely source locations derived from graph structure. The model is trained on simulated spreading outcomes and outputs a binary per-node label. As noted by Sterchi et al. \cite{Sterchi2025}, the multi-source formulation introduces important differences from the single-source problem studied in this thesis, including a sigmoid output layer that treats nodes independently rather than competing through a softmax normalisation.

Shah et al. \cite{Shah2020} evaluated both GCN and Graph Attention Network (GAT) variants for single-source detection. Their models take one-hot encoded SIR states as node features and output a softmax distribution over nodes, which is a cleaner formulation for single-source inference. They reported that GNNs outperform DMP on several benchmark networks, providing early evidence that learned message-passing can capture source-relevant structural patterns more effectively than hand-crafted probabilistic approximations.

Subsequent work introduced a range of architectural modifications. The MCGNN of Shu et al. employs a multi-channel architecture in which a line graph representation provides edge-level features alongside the original node graph, aiming to capture higher-order structural information. However, Sterchi et al. \cite{Sterchi2025} note that MCGNN incorrectly formulates single-source detection as a multi-label problem with sigmoid activations, undermining its comparability with softmax-based methods. The Infected Graph Convolutional Network (IGCN) proposed by Guo et al. introduces state-dependent aggregation weights, so that the influence of a neighbour on the central node's embedding is modulated by that neighbour's SIR state; this design reflects the intuition that infected and recovered nodes should contribute differently to source inference. The SD-STGCN of Sha et al. extends the input to multiple temporal snapshots of the spreading process, which is a more realistic observation model, but the underlying network is still treated as a static projection.

The systematic review and benchmark of Sterchi, Brack, and Hilfiker \cite{Sterchi2025} provides the most comprehensive evaluation of GNN-based source detection methods to date. Their study re-implements representative methods under controlled conditions and evaluates them against classical baselines on a common set of network topologies and epidemic parameters. The key empirical finding is that GNNs substantially and consistently outperform classical methods including DMP, SME, and the Jordan centre. The review also identifies several methodological concerns with the existing literature: claims of model-agnosticism are often made without empirical verification across different spreading models; the SME baseline is frequently absent or configured with an inadequate number of Monte Carlo samples, making comparisons unreliable; and the effect of training set size on GNN performance is understudied. Crucially for this thesis, Sterchi et al. explicitly note that all surveyed GNN methods, with the sole exception of Ru et al.'s Backtracking Network, operate on static projections of the contact network, discarding temporal information entirely.

## 3.3 GNN-Based Methods on Temporal Networks

The Backtracking Network (BN) of Ru et al. \cite{Ru2023} represents the current state of the art for GNN-based source detection on temporal contact networks and is the primary reference point for this thesis. Rather than projecting the temporal network to a static graph, the BN retains the full temporal activation structure by attaching to each edge in the aggregated graph a binary vector — referred to as an edge texture — that records at which discrete time steps the contact was active. A kernel-based message-passing operator \cite{Gilmer2017}\cite{Simonovsky2017} interleaves edge updates, in which edge hidden states are updated using the hidden state of the source node, with node updates, in which each node aggregates updated messages from all incoming edges. The architecture also incorporates expert knowledge by masking susceptible nodes to zero detection probability prior to the output softmax, reflecting the logical impossibility of a susceptible node being the source. Ru et al. demonstrate that the BN substantially outperforms classical baselines and show robustness to missing links and partially observed node states. The method is described in full technical detail in Chapter 5 of this thesis, as it serves as the temporal GNN baseline against which the De Bruijn GNN and the TEG-DAG-GNN are compared.

Beyond source detection, Rossi et al. proposed Temporal Graph Networks (TGNs) as a general-purpose framework for deep learning on dynamic graphs \cite{Rossi2020}. TGNs maintain a per-node memory state updated by a gated recurrent mechanism as new events arrive, and combine this memory with graph attention over current neighbourhood events. While TGNs have not been applied to source detection, they represent the broader architectural context within which temporal GNN methods for source detection should be understood: the core challenge is how to encode time-varying edge activity in a way that is informative for node-level classification tasks.

The De Bruijn Graph Neural Network (DBGNN) of Qarkaxhija et al. \cite{Qarkaxhija2022} offers a fundamentally different approach to temporal information. Rather than encoding temporal activity as edge features on a static graph, the DBGNN constructs a higher-order De Bruijn graph whose nodes correspond to ordered sequences of contacts, thereby directly representing causal walks and non-Markovian path statistics. This architecture provides an explicit inductive bias toward the causal path structure of temporal spreading processes, which the edge texture approach of the BN can only approximate implicitly. The Temporal Event Graph (TEG) framework of Saramäki et al. \cite{Saramaki2019} provides yet another structural representation: it maps a temporal network into a directed acyclic graph whose nodes are contact events and whose directed edges encode time-respecting sequential relationships. Applying the DAG convolutional network of Rey et al. \cite{Rey2025} to a TEG yields a method that processes the causal structure of the spreading process directly in the spectral domain of an acyclic graph.

## 3.4 Summary and Research Gap

The existing literature reveals a clear asymmetry: while classical source detection methods have been developed for both static and temporal network settings, GNN-based approaches have been almost entirely confined to static projections. The BN of Ru et al. is the only published GNN method designed specifically for temporal contact networks, and it has not been compared against alternative temporal GNN architectures embodying different inductive biases. No study has systematically evaluated whether representing temporal structure as edge activation patterns (BN), as higher-order causal walks (DBGNN), or as a directed acyclic event graph (TEG-DAG-GNN) yields materially different detection accuracy, or under what conditions each approach is superior. This thesis fills that gap by implementing all three architectures within a unified experimental framework and evaluating them against each other and against static GNN and classical baselines.
