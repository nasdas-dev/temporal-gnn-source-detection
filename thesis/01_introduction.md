# Chapter 1: Introduction

## 1.1 Motivation

Identifying the origin of an epidemic — the so-called "patient zero" — is one of the central problems at the intersection of network science and public health. When a pathogen emerges, reconstructing its point of introduction can inform containment strategies, guide contact-tracing efforts, and ultimately limit the scale of an outbreak. The societal stakes are high: the COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated that delays in pinpointing an epidemic's geographic and individual origin translate directly into uncontrolled community transmission \cite{WHO2020}. Similar imperatives arise in veterinary epidemiology, where the rapid identification of index farms during foot-and-mouth disease outbreaks has been shown to be critical for preventing national-scale livestock crises \cite{Keeling2001}. Beyond infectious disease, structurally analogous source-detection problems appear in the spread of misinformation and rumours on online social networks, where tracing a viral piece of content back to its origin is both a technical and a social challenge \cite{Shah2011}.

The increasing availability of high-resolution contact data has made computational approaches to this problem tractable. Bluetooth proximity sensors in hospital settings, RFID badges in conference environments, and digitised livestock movement records all yield datasets in which individual contacts are timestamped to the minute or second. These temporal contact networks carry strictly more information than the static projections traditionally used in network epidemiology: the ordering and timing of contacts governs which transmission chains are causally possible, a fact that static aggregation systematically discards.

## 1.2 Background and the Gap in Existing Work

Epidemic processes are naturally modelled as spreading processes on networks. An infected node transmits the pathogen to its susceptible neighbours according to a stochastic process; the outcome is an observed snapshot of infected, recovered, and susceptible individuals. Given only this final snapshot and the underlying contact structure, the task is to infer the node most likely to have initiated the outbreak. Early analytical work by Shah and Zaman introduced rumour centrality as a maximum likelihood estimator for the source of a spreading process on tree-structured networks \cite{Shah2011}. This sparked a productive tradition: the Jordan centre was proposed as a tractable approximation for general network topologies \cite{Shah2011}, dynamic message-passing (DMP) extended the framework to arbitrary spreading parameters \cite{Lokhov2014}, and the Soft Margin Estimator (SME) introduced a simulation-based approach that quantifies source likelihood by comparing observed infection snapshots with Monte Carlo realisations \cite{AntulovFantulin2015}. Loopy belief propagation offered a probabilistically principled alternative for networks with cycles \cite{Braunstein2016}.

Despite the empirical availability of temporal contact data, the overwhelming majority of these methods were designed for static networks, treating the contact structure as a fixed graph. Temporal networks — which record the precise timing and ordering of contacts — are a more realistic substrate. Several methods have been adapted or developed for this setting: Lokhov et al. extended dynamic message-passing to sequences of contact snapshots \cite{Lokhov2014}, and Rocha and Masuda proposed individual-based approximations for temporal spreading processes \cite{Rocha2016}. Nevertheless, the structured temporal ordering of contacts — which determines the set of causally valid transmission paths — has largely been treated as a second-order consideration rather than a primary modelling target.

Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have recently emerged as a powerful and data-driven approach to source detection, reframing the problem as supervised classification trained on simulated epidemic outcomes \cite{Dong2019}\cite{Shah2020}. A comprehensive review and benchmark by Sterchi, Brack, and Hilfiker established that GNN-based methods substantially outperform classical analytical and simulation-based baselines across a range of network topologies and epidemic parameters \cite{Sterchi2025}. Critically, however, that study also identifies a fundamental limitation of the field: virtually all GNN methods for source detection operate on static projections of the contact network, collapsing temporal information before any learning takes place. Sterchi et al. explicitly note that Ru et al.'s Backtracking Network is the sole published exception \cite{Ru2023}. There is currently no systematic study comparing multiple temporal GNN architectures, each embodying a distinct inductive bias about how temporal structure should be represented, against each other and against both static GNN and classical baselines on temporal contact networks.

## 1.3 This Thesis

This thesis addresses that gap by developing and evaluating three GNN architectures specifically designed to exploit temporal contact information for epidemic source detection. The first is the **Backtracking Network (BN)** proposed by Ru et al. \cite{Ru2023}, which operates on the aggregated static graph augmented with binary edge activation patterns — binary vectors recording at which time steps each contact was active — thereby encoding temporal structure as edge features while preserving the scalability of standard graph convolution. The second is the **De Bruijn Graph Neural Network (DBGNN)** proposed by Qarkaxhija et al. \cite{Qarkaxhija2022}, which constructs higher-order De Bruijn graph representations to explicitly model causal walks and non-Markovian temporal dynamics that cannot be captured by first-order aggregation. The third is a **DAG-GNN on Temporal Event Graphs (TEG)**, combining the event-graph representation of Saramäki et al. \cite{Saramaki2019} — which maps a temporal network into a directed acyclic graph whose nodes are contact events and whose edges encode time-respecting paths — with the DAG convolutional network (DCN) of Rey et al. \cite{Rey2025}, designed to perform spectral convolution on such acyclic structures. A static GNN operating on the time-aggregated contact graph serves as the primary baseline.

Training data for all models is generated via Monte Carlo SIR simulations using an adapted version of Holme's fast C-based temporal SIR simulation engine \cite{Holme2020}, and all experiments are tracked using the Weights and Biases framework.

Three research hypotheses guide the experimental evaluation. H1 (Causal Path Integrity) posits that temporal GNN architectures provide the highest marginal accuracy gain in structured networks where temporal ordering strictly limits the reachable set of nodes. H2 (Granularity Convergence) posits that the advantage of temporal architectures diminishes as the temporal resolution of the input decreases, with accuracy converging toward the static baseline as time-bin width increases. H3 (Temporal Signal-to-Noise Resilience) posits that temporal GNNs maintain higher accuracy than static models in late-stage outbreaks by pruning the combinatorial explosion of plausible transmission paths present in the aggregated contact history.

## 1.4 Contributions

This thesis makes the following contributions. First, we provide a unified re-implementation of the Backtracking Network of Ru et al. within a common experimental framework, enabling controlled comparison. Second, we adapt the De Bruijn GNN of Qarkaxhija et al. and the DAG convolutional network of Rey et al. to the source detection task, which has not been done in prior work. Third, we conduct the first systematic multi-architecture comparison of temporal GNN methods for source detection against each other and against classical baselines under identical data and evaluation conditions. Fourth, we analyse the robustness of each architecture to missing link observations and partial node-state information.
